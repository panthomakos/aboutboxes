About Boxes
 1998-2005


In 1997 I was reading The Poetics of Space and was inspired as I followed Gaston Bachelard on his psychological tour of the house. He explored the meaning of entering attics and basements, of opening cupboards and cabinets. He examined the significance of contained space. At some point I wrote "Idea - Boxes" on a scrap of paper.

The box is a powerful metaphor. A box holds promise, memory, and mystery. Opening a box is a discovery. It releases joy and surprise.

"What do you think of when you think of boxes?" I asked my international middle school art students in the fall of 1998. Their answers to this and other questions resulted in an extraordinary experience. For the next ten weeks they collectively explored the poetic potential of contained space. Using a holistic process of investigation, the students created unique three-dimensional boxes.

The results were astonishing.

We started by brainstorming. Everyone shared ideas for what a box is or could be. As students borrowed and expanded on each other's concepts, their thinking evolved and their ideas became more enigmatic and intriguing. The atmosphere began to buzz with curiosity and excitement.

We met once a day for a forty-five minute class period. Students shaped their ideas into rough draft test models and then into final 3D forms. Making a box was harder than it seemed at first. Students had to fit sections together, construct mobile parts, and integrate sculptural forms. Their ideas determined the materials they used.

Students designed, drew, and painted. They cut cardboard, twisted wire, and sculpted wood and papier-mache. They carved wax, stitched cloth, and knotted string. They fashioned movable and spring activated devices like hinges, latches, and handles. They constructed doors and drawers. They worked with illusion, suspending forms in space. Their thinking was challenged and stretched.

They took risks, failed, and had to start all over again. At some point everyone got discouraged and wanted to quit. Advice and encouragement from others helped students evaluate, revise, and succeed. They learned to see mistakes as positive forces that can lead in new directions towards new discoveries. They learned to stay open to experience. They realized they were capable of wondrous things. They became courageous and aware. They opened their eyes a little wider. They began to ask, "What else is possible?"

The finished boxes were marvelous and an impressive surprise. It was astounding that middle school students were capable of such sophisticated creative work. When students reflected on their experience, they were amazed that there were so many different ways to think about a box. They were impressed that making art required so much patience and hard work. And they were dazzled. The open-ended nature of the project gave students the freedom to experiment and explore. "It was like entering a new world," one student wrote, "a world of wonders, of things waiting to be discovered."

For the next seven years, my high school art students continued to make boxes. We approached a different initial question in a new way each year.

Boxes is the original middle school project.
The Culture Box is an exploration of cultural identity in a global world.
Magic Boxes is an investigation of mysteries of illusion and the mind.
The World in a Box is a view of the world through each student's window of perception.
One's Self Box is a search for an understanding of the individual within the larger context of human existence.
Form and Function Boxes is an examination of the relationship between a container and what it is made to contain.
The Interactive Box is an invitation to the viewer to open the box and actively participate.
